I worked on a project called Adaptive Context GraphRAG, and one of the biggest things I learned from it was not just about RAG itself, but about how easy it is to build on an idea before fully understanding its assumptions. At the beginning, I followed the project direction because one of my teammates already had experience with RAG, while I was still new to the area. But as we kept analyzing our own method, I started to see several theoretical and evaluation problems in what we were doing. Even though the project had flaws, it became a very valuable learning experience because it pushed me to think more critically about where GraphRAG works well and where it breaks down. 

The core idea of the project was to improve GraphRAG by addressing what we saw as a weakness in Leiden-based community detection. GraphRAG uses Leiden to partition a knowledge graph into communities that can later be summarized and retrieved efficiently. Our concern was that Leiden makes hard partitions based on graph structure, not semantic meaning, so semantically related nodes could be separated if they were only weakly connected in the graph. To fix that, we proposed adding a small number of “context nodes” from outside a community based on embedding similarity and graph proximity. In other words, we tried to enrich communities after partitioning so that they would capture more semantic connections. 

But as I studied the method more carefully, I realized this approach had a major theoretical contradiction. Leiden is already optimizing a structural objective, especially under CPM, where communities are supposed to be dense enough to justify being grouped together. So if Leiden separates a node from a community, that separation is not random—it reflects the graph structure and objective we chose. By later reintroducing external nodes using an embedding-based heuristic, we were partially undoing the same partition we had just treated as principled. That made me question whether we were really improving the method, or just patching over cuts we did not like with a second objective that was never fully justified. 

Another issue I came to care about was the lack of statistical validation. Our method added nodes when they looked similar enough and close enough, but we never established whether that similarity was actually meaningful or could just happen by chance. Without a null model, a significance test, or even a reference threshold, the score had no real grounding. That means the system could keep adding nodes simply because embeddings in dense graphs often show moderate similarity, even when the relationship is weak or accidental. Over time, that leads to context inflation: larger, noisier, less precise context instead of better semantic recovery. This was an important lesson for me, because it showed how a method can sound intuitive but still lack scientific support. 

I also learned to be much more careful about what embeddings actually represent. In this project, Node2Vec played a central role, and one mistake I identified was treating Node2Vec similarity as if it were semantic similarity. But Node2Vec does not read text or meaning at all—it only learns from graph structure through random walks. So when two nodes are close in Node2Vec space, that tells us they occupy similar structural positions or share graph neighborhoods, not that they mean similar things in language. Once I understood that more clearly, I realized that using Node2Vec to claim semantic improvement was a category error. 

That concern became even more serious when I looked at the evaluation. We used Node2Vec-based measures like Precision@k and cross-cluster similarity to evaluate a system that had already used Node2Vec to construct the communities. So the evaluation was circular: we were measuring success using the same signal that shaped the method in the first place. Of course it would look good under that lens. A stronger evaluation would have required something independent, like SBERT, downstream QA performance, or human judgment. Seeing this made me more aware of how easy it is to accidentally design an evaluation that confirms your own assumptions instead of testing them. 

Finally, I came away with a broader view on GraphRAG itself. I do think graphs can be useful, especially when interpretability and structure matter, but I no longer think graph-based RAG should be the first solution people reach for. The indexing pipeline is expensive, summarization is costly, and adding overlapping context nodes makes that even worse by increasing redundancy at both indexing time and retrieval time. My conclusion from this project is not that GraphRAG is useless, but that it needs a much stronger justification than people sometimes assume. For me personally, this project was valuable because it taught me how to question my own methods, separate structural signals from semantic ones, and treat negative findings as part of serious learning instead of failure.
