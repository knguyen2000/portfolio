I was born and raised in Vung Tau, Vietnam, and a big part of my story is how travel, study, and work across different countries shaped the way I see the world. My first big experiences abroad were in New Zealand and Japan when I was in high school. Those trips opened my eyes to new cultures, helped me become more independent, and made me excited about connecting with people from very different backgrounds. Later, moving to Finland for my bachelor’s degree at Hame University of Applied Sciences was one of the biggest turning points in my life. Living there taught me resilience, independence, and how much I enjoy international environments and friendships across cultures. A short exchange program in Hungary strengthened that even more, because I saw how much better teamwork can be when people bring different perspectives. 

When COVID started, I moved back to Vietnam and worked for two years as a software engineer at Netcompany. There, I contributed to large, meaningful systems across different countries, including projects in finance, government, customs, and secure digital communication. That experience taught me responsibility and showed me that technology can connect people across borders to solve important real-world problems. A business trip to Copenhagen especially stayed with me, because it made me realize how much I had grown—from being a student visiting the city to returning as a young professional. 

Now I am pursuing my master’s degree at the University of Virginia, where my interests have shifted more deeply toward AI research. At first, I came mainly to build practical skills for an industry job, but through courses in graph machine learning, NLP, and research seminars, I became more interested in the deeper questions behind AI. I started thinking seriously about problems like hallucination, bias, trustworthiness, alignment, and explainability. I realized I do not just want to build powerful systems—I want to help build systems that people can actually trust. At the same time, graduate school also taught me that failure is not something to avoid, but often the thing that pushes me to understand problems more deeply. 

Overall, I see myself as someone shaped by movement, curiosity, and persistence. I have traveled to 17 countries so far, and those experiences have made me love meeting new people, learning how others live, and growing through unfamiliar situations. Outside of academics and work, I enjoy hiking, going to the gym, and cooking. Cooking especially feels like a reflection of how I approach life: trial and error, learning from mistakes, and trying again until something meaningful comes out of it.
